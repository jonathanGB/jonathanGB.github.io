à propos

Je suis présentement à ma 2e année, et je me dirige vers ma 3e cet hiver (étant donné des crédits du Cégep). Je cherche des opportunités co-op pour l'été prochain dans les domaines du développement web et de la robotique/IoT.

Présentement, je suis VP Social et Communications à l'Association des étudiant(e)s en génie logiciel à uOttawa. Je fais partie de cette organisation depuis ma première année, dans laquelle j'ai travaillé principalement sur la traduction des communications de l'anglais au français et sur l'annonce aux étudiants des événements organisés par l'association.

À l'extérieur de l'école, j'aime lire sur l'histoire, les sciences politiques et la technologie, que ce soit dans des livres, dans des journaux, ou sur HackerNews et Medium. Je suis des cours en ligne sur des plateformes comme edX et Codecademy dans le but d'apprendre de nouvelles technologies, de nouveaux langages de programmation ou simplement par intérêt sur des sujets liés au génie/science.

De plus, j'aime jouer au hockey (je suis un défenseur), regarder le soccer (fan de l'Allemagne) et jouer de la batterie. Mes groupes de musique préférés sont Rush (vous savez... Tom Sawyer? Ou si vous osez, 2112... ouais, ça dure 20mins, mais c'est ça qui est ça!), Nirvana (et pas mal tout le grunge/rock alternatif des années 90), ainsi que Jean Leloup.

talents

Je ne peux pas penser à une meilleure manière de catégoriser mes talents en technologie/programmation que celui proposé par Craig Bryan, un collègue de l'AÉGL. Le système est séparé en «famille» (Family), «Amis» (Friends) et «Connaissances» (Acquaintances), tel qu'expliqué ci-dessous (laissé dans sa forme originale):

My "Family" — I'm in contact with these folks almost every day and I know them very well, including their quirks.
My "Friends" — I see these people pretty often, and I enjoy spending time with them. They keep some secrets from me, and that's just fine.
My "Acquaintances" — I see these people around, and only have long conversations with them occasionally. Some of them are old friends with whom I've fallen out of touch, and others are those with whom I wish I could spend more time.
Donc voilà!

  • Polymer Bank Notes pages

  • TSI Mobile

    Full-stack Webapp ~~ JavaScript & jQuery - AWS - PHP - cURL
    October 2014 - November 2014

    I developed a webapp for VIA rail Canada that could show in real-time if a specific train line or trains going to a specific station were on-time or late. This was done by showing "Scheduled Arrival" time compared to the "Estimated Arrival" time, and the ETA of a train for a certain station. All the information used was inside XML files, which were grabbed on the back-end using cron and cURL, then parsed. The app would update data via AJAX calls every minute.

    On the VIA side, some analytics could definitely be useful. I wrote a PHP script to grab data and store it inside a database, in order to have an on-time performance chart of the day for every hour of service. Also, I made a grid with every train on service in which we could easily visualize the on-time performance of all of them.

    This was a great experience to build a full-stack webapp: I had to create an EC2 instance on AWS, learn how to connect to remote servers via SSH, get used to the Linux terminal and tools (like cron), and install web dependencies.

  • Mobile Robot

    Physics project ~~ Arduino - Electronics
    GitHub repo
    February 2014 - April 2014

    Part of my program in Cégep was to work on a science project during our last semester, which I decided to do in the physics department. With the help of a teammate, both having no background in electronics (and me having basic programming knowledge in JavaScript), we decided to build an autonomous mobile robot using an Arduino.

    My teammate focused more on the circuity, while I worked on the programming. The robot consisted of 2 independently controlled DC motors, an Arduino, a distance sensor, a collision sensor, a RGB LED, a dual H-Bridge, and an alimentation of 7.5V (for the Arduino) and of 12V (for the motors). The program was constantly reading data from the sensors, doing basic filtering of the data acquired and then outputting a specific voltage to the motors and triggering a specific colour of the RGB LED.

    It was a very inspiring experience, convincing me to go in Software Engineering. Not only that, but our project was rewarded with the best science project presentation of the day (here's the poster, in French only!)

    If you're curious to learn more about the robot, the GitHub repo contains the code used, a brief presentation and also videos of the robot and of the collision sensor we used. The videos are in French, but I put up a description of them here!
