1.3. From Wikipedia:

In mathematical optimization, statistics, econometrics, decision theory, machine learning and computational neuroscience, a loss function or cost function is a function that maps an event or values of one or more variables onto a real number intuitively representing some "cost" associated with the event. An optimization problem seeks to minimize a loss function.

We've generated a cost function on parameters $x,y \in \mathbb{R}$ $L(x,y)= 3x^2y - y^3 - 3x^2 - 3y^2 + 2$. Find the critical points (optima) of $L(x,y)$.

ANSWER

To find the critical points, we first need to calculate the partial derivatives of $L$ in $x$ and $y$.

$\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = L_x = 6yx - 6x \quad \quad \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = L_y = 3x^2 - 3y^2 - 6y$

Then we want to see at which points $L_x = L_y = 0$

From $L_x$:

$$6yx - 6x = 0$$

$$yx - x = 0$$

$$yx = x \quad (1)$$

From (1), we can establish the following constraints to satisfy our system:

  1. If $x \neq 0$, then $y = 1$
  2. If $y \neq 1$, then $x = 0$

Let's narrow our solution by applying these two constraints to our second equation $L_y$.

1- First case (constraint): if $y = 1$

$$L_y = 3x^2 - 3y^2 - 6y = 0$$

$$3x^2 - 3 - 6 = 0$$

$$3x^2 - 9 = 0$$

$$x^2 = 3$$

$$x = \pm\sqrt{3}$$

Therefore, we know we have two critical points at $(\sqrt{3}, 1)$ and $(-\sqrt{3}, 1)$.

2- Second case: if $x = 0$

$$L_y = 3x^2 - 3y^2 - 6y = 0$$

$$-3y^2 - 6y = 0$$

$$y^2 + 2y = 0$$

$$(y + 1)^2 = 1$$

$$y + 1 = \pm1$$

$y = 0$ and $y = -2$ are both valid, so we have two other critical points: $(0, 0)$ and $(0, -2)$.

We have four critical points, but we don't know yet if they are local maxima/minima, or saddle points. To do that, we must build the Hessian matrix.

$L_{xx} = 6y - 6 \quad \quad L_{yy} = 6y - 6 \quad \quad L_{xy} = 6x$

$$H(x,y) = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 6y - 6 & 6x \\ 6x & 6y - 6 \end{array} \right]$$.

$$\det H(x,y) = 36 \, (y^2 - x^2 - 2y + 1)$$

  • $\det H(\sqrt{3}, 1) = -108 < 0$, so $(\sqrt{3}, 1)$ is a saddle point

  • $\det H(-\sqrt{3}, 1) = -108 < 0$, so $(-\sqrt{3}, 1)$ is a saddle point

  • $\det H(0, 0) = 36 > 0$ and $L_{xx}(0, 0) = -6 < 0$, so $(0,0)$ is a local maximum

  • $\det H(0, -2) = 324 > 0$ and $L_{xx}(0, -2) = -18 < 0$, so $(0,-2)$ is a local maximum

1.4. A central aspect of call center operations is the per minute statistics of caller demographics. Because of the massive call volumes call centers achieve, these per minute statistics can often take on well-known distributions. In the CS109 Homework Helpdesk, X and Y are discrete random variables with X measuring the number of female callers per minute and Y the total number of callers per minute. We've determined historically the joint pmf of (X, Y) and found it to be $$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = e^{-4}\frac{2^y}{x!(y-x)!}$$ where $y \in \mathbb{N}, x \in [0, y]$ (That is to say the total number of callers in a minute is a non-negative integer and the number of female callers naturally assumes a value between 0 and the total number of callers inclusive). Find the mean and variance of the marginal distribution of $X$.

Hints:

  1. $x \in \left[0,y\right] \Rightarrow x < y$.
  2. You may find the change of variable $z = y-x$ helpful.
  3. Recall: $$ \sum_{z=0}^{\infty}\frac{2^{z}}{z!} =e^{2}$$

ANSWER

We want the marginal distribution in $x$

$$p_X(x) = \sum_{y \in R_y} p_{XY}(x,y)$$ where $R_y$ is the range of $y$, so $R_y = \{y \in \mathbb{N} : y \geq x\}$

$$p_X(x) = \sum_{y \in R_y} \frac{e^{-4} 2^y}{x! (y - x)!} = \frac{e^{-4}}{x!} \sum_{y \in R_y} \frac{2^y}{(y-x)!}$$

Suppose $z = y -x$

$$2^y = 2^{z+x} = 2^z \cdot 2^x$$

The range of $z$ ($R_z$) is the set $\mathbb{N}$

$$p_X(x) = \frac{e^{-4}}{x!} \sum_{z=0}^{\infty} \frac{2^z \cdot 2^x}{z!} = \frac{2^x \cdot e^{-4}}{x!} \sum_{z=0}^{\infty} \frac{2^z}{z!} = \frac{2^x \cdot e^{-4}}{x!} \cdot e^2$$

$$p_X(x) = \frac{2^x}{x! \cdot e^2}$$

This distribution looks oddly like a Poisson distribution, which is defined as

$$poisson_X(x) = \frac{\lambda^x}{x! \cdot e^{\lambda}}$$

where $\lambda = 2$.

It just happens that the mean and the variance in a Poisson distribution is equal to $\lambda$, so the mean and variance of the marginal distribution of $X$ is 2.

Does the system have any solutions?

We can prove that a system of linear equations has no solutions if Gauss-Jordan operations lead to an inconsistent equation.

\begin{align*} [A \, | \, b] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 3 & 0 & 3\\ 0 & 1 & \frac{1}{2} & -1\\ 2 & 0 & -3 & 0 \end{array} \right] \end{align*}

1.7 It is very often the case that we are not dealing with square matrices (that is $m \neq n$). Yet we still want to find solutions to the system of equations. Consider \begin{align*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \\ -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} and \begin{align*} b = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \\ c \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} where $c \in \mathbb{R}$.

Is there a solution to this system? When?

ANSWER

\begin{align*} [A \, | \, b] = \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 3 & -1\\ -1 & 3 & c \end{array} \right] \end{align*}

\begin{align*} = \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 2 & 3 & -1\\ 0 & 5 & c + 3 \end{array} \right] \quad L_3 \leftarrow L_3 + L_1 \end{align*}

\begin{align*} = \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 0 & -1 & -7\\ 0 & 5 & c + 3 \end{array} \right] \quad L_2 \leftarrow L_2 - 2L_1 \end{align*}

\begin{align*} = \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 0 & 1 & 7\\ 0 & 5 & c + 3 \end{array} \right] \quad L_2 \leftarrow -L_2 \end{align*}

\begin{align*} = \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 3\\ 0 & 1 & 7\\ 0 & 0 & c - 32 \end{array} \right] \quad L_3 \leftarrow L_3 - 5L_2 \end{align*}

\begin{align*} = \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 0 & -11\\ 0 & 1 & 7\\ 0 & 0 & c - 32 \end{array} \right] \quad L_1 \leftarrow L_1 - 2L_2 \end{align*}

We have reduced our system, and we get the following equations:

$$\chi_1 = -11 \quad \chi_2 = 7 \quad 0 = c - 32$$

Therefore, if $c = 32$, $0 = 0$, so we have one solution: $\chi_1 = -11, \chi_2 = 7$. If $c \neq 32$, then we have $0 \neq 0$, which is impossible, so we have no solutions.

1.8 37 of the 76 female Computer Science concentrators have taken Data Science 1 (DS1) while 50 of the 133 male concentrators haven taken DS1. Perform a statistical test to determine if interest in Data Science (by taking DS1) is related to gender. Be sure to state your conclusion.

ANSWER

We have two qualitative variables, so we will use the chi-square test to determine whether or not there is a relation between the gender of the students and the interest in data science.

We enounce $H_0$: interest in data science is not related to gender. The alternative hypothesis, $H_1$, is that interest in data science is in fact related to gender. To prove statistically that there is a relation, we must have a certain level of confidence to reject $H_0$, hence assuming $H_1$ is likely enough. This parameter, $\alpha$, is usually of 5%, meaning that we assume 5% risk of wrongly rejecting $H_0$.

Let's build a table with the numbers we are given to compare the observed values to the expected values under $H_0$.

Takes DS1 Expected (under Ho) Does not take DS1 Expected (under Ho) Marginal sum
Male 50 133 * 87 / 209 = 55.4 83 133 * 122 / 209 = 77.6 133
Female 37 76 * 87 / 209 = 31.6 39 76 * 122 / 209 = 44.4 76
Marginal sum 87 87 122 122 209

We want to calculate $\chi^2_{observed}$.

$$\chi^2_{observed} = \sum_i^{rows} \sum_j^{cols} \frac{(Observed_{ij} - Expected_{ij})^2}{Expected_{ij}}$$

$$\chi^2_{observed} = \frac{(50 - 55.4)^2}{55.4} + \frac{(83 - 77.6)^2}{77.6} + \frac{(37 - 31.6)^2}{31.6} + \frac{(39 - 44.4)^2}{44.4} = 2.49$$

The degree of freedom in our situation is $(2-1) \cdot (2-1) = 1$. Now, if we look in a pre-calculated chi-square table, the cell at the intersection of 1 degree of freedom with $\alpha = 5\%$ is 3.841. Our $\chi^2_{observed} = 2.49$ is way below that threshold, therefore we cannot reasonably reject $H_0$, hence we cannot statistically state that interest in data science is related to gender in the provided statistical population. In fact, the risk factor $\alpha$ necessary to reject $H_0$ would be between 10% and 20% according to the pre-calculated chi-square table, which is not significative at all.

We can safely assume that interest in data science is therefore not related to gender.

Sequences and Series

Many mathematical and physical constants can be expressed as infinite sums, infinite products, and limits of recursion relationships. In this problem, we will consider representations for two very famous numbers.

$\pi$

One way to represent $\pi$ is with the infinite sum \begin{align*} \frac{\pi^{2}}{6} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}{\frac{1}{n^{2}}}. \end{align*}

$\varphi$: the golden ratio¶

The golden ratio $\varphi$ is the positive root of the quadratic equation $x^{2} - x - 1 = 0$. It's exact value is $\varphi = \dfrac{1}{2}\left(1 + \sqrt{5}\right) \approx 1.608033\ldots$. Interestingly, it can also be expressed as the limit of the ratio of consecutive terms in the Fibonacci sequance. The $n^{\textrm{th}}$ term of the Fibonacci sequence is given by

\begin{align*} F_{n} = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}, \quad n > 2 \end{align*}
where $F_{1} = F_{2} = 1$ are seed values. For examle, the ratio $F_{7} / F_{6} = 1.625$.

In this problem, you will write a function to compute $\pi$ and $\varphi$ from the representations just described.

def numbers(number, precision):
    if not isinstance(precision, int) or precision < 3:
        raise ValueError("the second parameter (precision) must be an integer bigger than 2")

    if number != "pi" and number != "golden":
        raise ValueError("the first parameter (number) must be either \"pi\" or \"golden\"")

    result = calculatePi(precision) if number == "pi" else calculateGolden(precision)
    
    print("Result of {} with {} terms is: {}".format(number, precision, result))
    return result
    
def calculatePi(term):
    value = 0
    
    for n in range(1, term):
        value += 1 / n**2
        
    return np.sqrt(value * 6)

def calculateGolden(term):
    cache = {
        1: 1,
        2: 1,
    }
    
    return calculateGolden_rec(term-1, cache) / calculateGolden_rec(term-2, cache)

def calculateGolden_rec(term, cache):
    if term not in cache:    
        cache[term] = calculateGolden_rec(term-1, cache) + calculateGolden_rec(term-2, cache)

    return cache[term]
